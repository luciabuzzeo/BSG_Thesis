Diploma Thesis Summary

Application of malting & brewing by-products into cereal-based products — enzymatic treatment, particle-size fractionation and digital image analysis of BSG-enriched wheat bread. Author: Lucia Buzzeo. Each field is within the character limit set by the form (count shown includes spaces). Use “Copy text” to paste into the portal.

Diploma work objectives 975 / 1000 chars

The general aim was to evaluate how xylanase enzymatic treatment and particle-size fractionation of brewer's spent grain (BSG) affect the techno-functional, compositional and sensory quality of BSG-enriched wheat bread, and to develop a reproducible digital image analysis (DIA) method for crumb structure, so as to identify a BSG formulation comparable to a conventional wheat-flour reference and support BSG valorisation. Specific objectives were: (1) characterise the proximate composition and wet gluten of flour, BSG and their mixtures; (2) assess dough rheology by farinograph and amylograph; (3) determine the effect of BSG level, xylanase dose (70/140 ppm) and particle size (unsieved vs ≤250 µm) on loaf volume and shape; (4) evaluate sensory acceptance and establish the inclusion threshold; (5) develop and apply BreadScan DIA to quantify crumb metrics; and (6) integrate all data to select the best formulation and validate DIA as a non-destructive quality proxy.

Material and methods of diploma work 1385 / 1500 chars

Samples were wheat breads (type T-650 flour) with BSG replacing flour at 10% and 20%: five formulations in Batch 1 (control; 10% and 20% BSG; 10% and 20% BSG + 70 ppm xylanase, all unsieved) and nine in Batch 2 crossing BSG type (unsieved vs ≤250 µm sieved fraction) with xylanase dose (70 and 140 ppm), plus a wheat control. BSG originated from the micro-malthouse of the Agribiotech Research Centre, Nitra (HEINEKEN Slovakia pale malt), dried at 60 °C, knife-milled and, for Batch 2, sieved to ≤250 µm. The enzyme was Pentopan Mono BG (Novozymes), a GH11 endo-1,4-β-xylanase from Aspergillus oryzae, added directly to the flour. Proximate composition followed AOAC methods (moisture 925.10; ash 923.03 at 550 °C; Kjeldahl protein 979.09, N×6.25; crude fibre 962.09) and wet gluten AACC 38-12.02. Dough rheology was measured by farinograph and amylograph (Batch 1); loaf volume by rapeseed displacement and shape by caliper (height/width); sensory acceptance by a trained eight-member panel (weighted 5-point, 100-point basis). Crumb structure was quantified with BreadScan, an in-house browser DIA pipeline (luminance greyscale; Sauvola thresholding for control and peak-mode for dark BSG crumb; n=3 cross-sections). Analyses were performed in the laboratories of the Faculty of Biotechnology and Food Sciences (Institute of Food Sciences), Slovak University of Agriculture in Nitra.

Results of diploma work 1080 / 1500 chars

  • BSG raised ash, protein and crude fibre in proportion to the substitution level, but diluted wet gluten from 29.0% in wheat flour to 22.5% at 20% BSG.
  • Loaf volume and 2D crumb porosity fell with BSG: porosity dropped from 41.5% (control) to 16–28% in BSG breads, and the crumb became finer and denser.
  • Sensory acceptance was dose-dependent: 10% stayed acceptable while 20% failed in both panel sessions, confirming a 10–15% inclusion threshold.
  • On unsieved BSG, 70 ppm xylanase raised loaf volume (+6.5% at 10%) but lowered the sensory score (76.75 → 65.25) — the Batch-1 paradox.
  • Increasing the dose to 140 ppm gave the highest volume of the study (1050 mL) but not better sensory quality, and in several cases worse.
  • Best formulation: 10% sieved BSG (≤250 µm) + 70 ppm xylanase — sensory 85.0 vs 85.9 for the control (lowest variability, CV 9.4%), volume 925 vs 975 mL, the highest porosity among BSG breads, and +14% protein / +30% ash.
  • BreadScan metrics tracked both loaf volume and sensory scores, supporting DIA as a non-destructive formulation-screening tool.

Conclusions of diploma work 2583 / 3000 chars

The three working hypotheses were confirmed. (H1) BSG impaired loaf volume, crumb porosity and sensory acceptance in a dose-dependent way, with a reproducible 10–15% threshold: 20% substitution exceeded consumer acceptance in two independent panels, while 10% remained acceptable on composition, technology and sensory grounds. (H2) Xylanase and particle-size fractionation act through distinct mechanisms: the enzyme reliably improved loaf volume (highest volume, 1050 mL, at 10% unsieved BSG + 140 ppm) but did not systematically improve sensory quality and worsened it when arabinoxylan substrate was limited, whereas sieving traded a small volume loss for clear gains in porosity, cell density and sensory acceptance. (H3) BreadScan DIA metrics correlated with technological and sensory outcomes, validating DIA as a non-destructive screening proxy, although crumb structure proved a necessary but not sufficient determinant of acceptance.

The best-performing formulation was 10% sieved BSG (≤250 µm) with 70 ppm xylanase: it matched the wheat-flour control in sensory acceptance (85.0 vs 85.9, within one standard deviation, with the lowest panel variability of the study), approached it in loaf volume (925 vs 975 mL) and shape, achieved the best crumb structure of the BSG breads, and delivered a nutritional gain (+14% protein, +30% ash). This resolves the Batch-1 paradox, in which xylanase on unsieved BSG improved volume but degraded flavour.

The principal conclusion is that sensory mitigation in BSG bread is governed primarily by particle-size optimisation rather than enzyme-dose escalation; xylanase remains a complementary technological lever, most useful at higher substitution where volume is limiting, with no benefit beyond 70 ppm at 10% inclusion.

For practice, this identifies a directly usable route to a protein-enriched, BSG-valorising wheat bread comparable to a conventional reference, and demonstrates DIA as a practical screening tool for fibre-enriched formulations. Limitations: the AOAC crude-fibre method underestimates soluble fibre and several Batch-2 values lay near its detection limit, and the eight-member panel limited statistical power. Further research should add soluble/insoluble dietary-fibre profiling, direct assay of free phenolics and reducing sugars to confirm the sensory mechanism, a larger panel with formal statistical testing, instrumental texture and shelf-life measurement, 3D crumb imaging (micro-CT), cross-device validation of BreadScan, and extension of valorisation to other brewing by-products such as malt rootlets.
