# Abstract

Brewer's spent grain (BSG) is the most abundant by-product of the brewing industry, representing roughly 85–90% of its solid residues and an estimated 36–39 million tonnes generated worldwide each year. Despite a nutritional profile rich in dietary fibre, protein, minerals and phenolic compounds, BSG is still largely landfilled or downgraded to low-value animal feed, in conflict with circular-economy and sustainability objectives. Its incorporation into bread offers a direct valorisation route, but the lignocellulosic fibre dilutes the gluten network, competes for water and impairs loaf volume, crumb structure and sensory acceptance at inclusion levels above 10–15%.

This thesis evaluated how two mitigation strategies — enzymatic treatment with the endo-1,4-β-xylanase Pentopan Mono BG and particle-size fractionation of BSG by sieving (≤250 µm) — affect the techno-functional and sensory quality of wheat bread enriched with BSG, and developed a reproducible digital image analysis (DIA) method to quantify crumb structure. The experimental work was carried out in two successive batches. Batch 1 established the baseline effect of 10% and 20% BSG substitution and of xylanase at 70 ppm on unsieved BSG, characterised by proximate composition, dough rheology (farinograph and amylograph), loaf volume, shape and trained-panel sensory evaluation. Batch 2 widened the formulation space to nine breads crossing BSG type (unsieved vs. ≤250 µm fraction) and xylanase dose (70 vs. 140 ppm) at 10% and 20% substitution, and added crumb-structure quantification through BreadScan, an in-house, browser-based DIA pipeline developed specifically for this work and adapted to the dark, fibre-laden crumb of BSG bread.

BSG incorporation increased ash, protein and fibre content while progressively reducing loaf volume, paste viscosity and 2D porosity, and shifted the crumb toward a finer, denser cell architecture. Sensory acceptance fell sharply above 10% substitution in both batches, confirming a consistent 10–15% threshold. Xylanase improved loaf volume but, when applied to unsieved BSG, degraded sensory scores; raising the dose to 140 ppm maximised volume yet did not systematically improve, and in several cases worsened, sensory acceptance. Particle-size fractionation emerged as the decisive sensory lever: the formulation combining 10% sieved BSG (≤250 µm) with 70 ppm xylanase matched the wheat-flour control in sensory acceptance (85.00 vs. 85.94 points, within one standard deviation, with the lowest panel variability of the study), approached it in loaf volume (925 vs. 975 mL) and crumb porosity (the highest among the BSG breads), and delivered a measurable nutritional gain (+14% protein, +30% ash relative to the control). DIA-derived metrics tracked both technological and sensory outcomes, validating BreadScan as a non-destructive screening tool. The study identifies 10% sieved BSG with 70 ppm xylanase as the best-performing formulation and concludes that sensory mitigation in BSG bread is governed primarily by particle-size optimisation, with xylanase acting as a complementary technological lever most useful at higher substitution levels.

**Keywords:** brewer's spent grain; bread; xylanase; particle-size fractionation; circular economy; digital image analysis; crumb porosity; dietary fibre.

# 1. Introduction

## 1.1 Brewing by-products and the circular economy

The brewing process is a sequence of biotechnological operations designed to convert cereal starches into fermentable sugars and, subsequently, into ethanol and carbon dioxide. Along the way it generates several solid co-products — malt rootlets during malting, brewer's spent grain (BSG) during mashing and lautering, spent hops after boiling, and spent yeast following fermentation — which together account for the majority of the industry's solid residues (Pasquet, Villain-Gambier, & Trébouet, 2024; Chetrariu & Dabija, 2023). Among these, BSG is by far the most abundant, representing approximately 85–90% of the total. Roughly 0.2 kg of wet BSG are produced per litre of beer, so that global beer output exceeding 1.86 billion hectolitres in 2023 corresponds to an estimated 36–39 million tonnes of BSG generated annually, of which Europe alone contributes some 6.4 million tonnes (Parchami, Ferreira, & Taherzadeh, 2021; Statista, 2024; European Commission, 2022).

The conventional fate of this material is problematic. Because of its high moisture content (around 80%) and rapid microbial spoilage, wet BSG is costly to store and transport, and is mostly distributed to farmers as low-value animal feed, converted to biogas, or sent to landfill; landfilling a single tonne is estimated to release 513 kg of CO₂ equivalent (European Commission, 2022). This underutilisation represents a missed opportunity within the framework of the circular economy, which emphasises the prolongation of material life cycles and the transformation of waste into resources (Geissdoerfer, Savaget, Bocken, & Hultink, 2017). Valorising BSG aligns directly with the European Union's Circular Economy Action Plan and Green Deal, and with the United Nations Sustainable Development Goals 12 and 13 on responsible production and climate action (European Commission, 2020; United Nations, 2015).

## 1.2 Brewer's spent grain as a functional food ingredient

From a nutritional standpoint, BSG is a compositionally rich material. It consists chiefly of the seed coat, pericarp and husk layers of the original barley grain and is a lignocellulosic matrix dominated by fibre (cellulose and hemicellulose), protein and lignin (Lynch, Steffen, & Arendt, 2016; Waters, Jacob, Titze, Arendt, & Zannini, 2012). Approximately half of its dry weight is dietary fibre, with arabinoxylans — the major non-starch polysaccharide of cereals — accounting for up to 40% of the dry matter, while protein may reach 30% and is notable for its content of essential amino acids such as lysine, typically limiting in cereals (Mussatto, Dragone, & Roberto, 2006; Ikram, Huang, Zhang, Wang, & Yin, 2017). BSG is additionally a relevant source of minerals and of phenolic compounds, principally ferulic and p-coumaric acids, which confer antioxidant capacity (Farcas, Socaciu, Tofana, & Mudura, 2014; Lynch, Steffen, & Arendt, 2016).

These constituents underpin a range of documented health benefits — fibre-mediated effects on cholesterol, glycaemic response and intestinal health, and the antioxidant and anti-inflammatory activity of its phenolics — and have motivated the exploration of BSG as an ingredient across many food categories, including bakery products, extruded snacks, meat products and value-added extracts (Chetrariu & Dabija, 2023; Baiano, Gatta, Rutigliano, & Fiore, 2023). Bread is among the most studied matrices, because incorporating BSG simultaneously increases dietary fibre and protein and reduces rapidly digestible starch, shifting the loaf toward a more nutritionally dense profile (Czubaszek, Wojciechowicz-Budzisz, Spychaj, & Kawa-Rygielska, 2022; Pérez-Alva, Martín-del-Campo, & Baigts-Allende, 2025).

## 1.3 Technological challenges and mitigation strategies in BSG-enriched bread

The same fibre fraction that makes BSG nutritionally attractive also compromises bread quality. The abundant hydroxyl groups of its cell-wall polysaccharides drive intense competition for water during dough formation, leaving less free water for gluten development and starch gelatinisation, while the barley proteins do not form an equivalent viscoelastic network; the result is a stiffer, less extensible dough that yields compact loaves of lower specific volume and denser crumb (Neylon, Arendt, & Zannini, 2020; Stojceska & Ainsworth, 2008; Pérez-Alva, Martín-del-Campo, & Baigts-Allende, 2025). Coarse, husk-derived particles further disrupt the gluten matrix and produce a gritty mouthfeel, and the release of bound phenolics contributes bitterness and astringency. Consequently, sensory acceptance declines in a dose-dependent manner, with the practical upper limit for conventional bread generally placed at 10–15% flour substitution (Ktenioudaki, Chaurin, Reis, & Gallagher, 2013; Pérez-Alva, Martín-del-Campo, & Baigts-Allende, 2025).

Two complementary mitigation strategies are central to this thesis. The first is particle-size fractionation: BSG is compositionally heterogeneous across sieve fractions, with the coarse (>250 µm) fraction concentrating cellulose- and lignin-rich husk and pericarp, while the finer fraction is enriched in arabinoxylan and protein. Reducing particle size by milling and sieving disperses the fibre more homogeneously, lessens disruption of the gluten network and improves crumb uniformity (Robertson et al., 2010; Ktenioudaki, Chaurin, Reis, & Gallagher, 2013). The second is enzymatic treatment with xylanase, which hydrolyses the β-(1→4)-xylan backbone of arabinoxylans, converting water-unextractable forms into soluble fragments that release bound water for gluten hydration and stabilise gas cells as hydrocolloids, thereby improving loaf volume and crumb softness (Bustos, Pérez, & León, 2020; Waters, Jacob, Titze, Arendt, & Zannini, 2012). However, xylanase action is dose-dependent and carries a recognised technological–sensory trade-off, since excessive hydrolysis liberates bitter phenolics and reducing sugars that can degrade flavour even as volume improves (Stojceska & Ainsworth, 2008).

## 1.4 Digital image analysis of crumb structure and the knowledge gap

Because the consequences of BSG enrichment are largely structural, they require objective, reproducible characterisation if formulations are to be compared on a common basis. The bread crumb is a foam-like porous solid whose internal architecture — the size, number, shape and distribution of gas cells — governs texture, mechanical strength and consumer perception (Scanlon & Zghal, 2001). Digital image analysis (DIA) converts a two-dimensional cross-section into quantitative descriptors such as void fraction, cell density, mean cell area and uniformity, offering a rapid, non-destructive alternative to bulk methods that cannot resolve cell populations (Gonzales-Barron & Butler, 2006; Zghal, Scanlon, & Sapirstein, 1999). Applying DIA to BSG bread is, however, non-trivial: the dark crumb colour reduces the intensity contrast between pores and matrix, and embedded fibre particles mimic small pores, so that thresholding protocols developed for white wheat bread cannot be transferred directly and must be adapted — for example by replacing bimodal-assumption methods with histogram peak-mode thresholding for dark crumbs (Bosakova-Ardenska & Danev, 2015; Sauvola & Pietikäinen, 2000).

Despite a substantial literature on the enzymatic and mechanical mitigation of BSG bread defects, and a separate body of work on DIA of conventional bread, no study has yet combined the two: the crumb structure of bread made from enzymatically treated and particle-size-fractionated BSG has not been characterised with a DIA method tailored to such formulations. Addressing this gap — by jointly assessing xylanase dose and particle-size fractionation and by developing a bread-type-adaptive DIA pipeline for objective crumb characterisation — is the central motivation of this thesis.

# 2. Aim and objectives

## 2.1 General aim

The general aim of this thesis is to evaluate how enzymatic treatment with xylanase and particle-size fractionation of brewer's spent grain affect the techno-functional, compositional and sensory quality of wheat bread enriched with BSG, and to develop a reproducible digital image analysis method capable of quantifying the resulting crumb structure. The overarching purpose is to identify a BSG-enriched bread formulation whose technological, structural and sensory quality is comparable to that of a conventional wheat-flour reference, thereby supporting the valorisation of BSG within a circular-economy framework.

## 2.2 Specific objectives

To achieve this aim, the following specific objectives were defined:

1. To characterise the proximate composition (moisture, ash, protein, crude fibre and wet gluten) of wheat flour, dried BSG and flour–BSG mixtures, and to quantify the compositional changes introduced by BSG substitution at 10% and 20%.

2. To assess the effect of BSG incorporation, and of xylanase treatment, on dough rheology by farinograph and amylograph analysis, relating water absorption, development time, stability and pasting behaviour to bread-making performance.

3. To determine the influence of BSG substitution level, xylanase dose (70 and 140 ppm) and BSG particle size (unsieved vs. a ≤250 µm sieved fraction) on the technological quality of the bread, measured as loaf volume and height-to-width ratio.

4. To evaluate the sensory acceptance of the formulations through a trained panel, and to establish the practical inclusion threshold and the relative contribution of enzymatic treatment and particle-size fractionation to sensory quality.

5. To develop and apply BreadScan, an in-house, browser-based digital image analysis pipeline adapted to the dark, fibre-laden crumb of BSG bread, and to quantify crumb-structure metrics (2D porosity, cell density and mean cell area) for each formulation.

6. To integrate the compositional, technological, sensory and structural results in order to identify the best-performing formulation and to assess the value of DIA as a non-destructive proxy for technological and sensory quality.

## 2.3 Working hypotheses

The experimental design was guided by three working hypotheses: (i) that BSG substitution would progressively impair loaf volume, crumb porosity and sensory acceptance in a dose-dependent manner, with a threshold near 10–15%; (ii) that xylanase treatment and particle-size fractionation would mitigate these defects through distinct mechanisms — the enzyme acting chiefly on the biochemical causes (water competition and dough rigidity) and sieving on the physical causes (gluten disruption and gritty mouthfeel) — and could act synergistically; and (iii) that DIA-derived crumb metrics would correlate with technological and sensory outcomes closely enough to serve as an objective screening tool.

# Conclusion

This thesis set out to evaluate enzymatic treatment with xylanase and particle-size fractionation as strategies for mitigating the quality defects of brewer's spent grain in wheat bread, and to develop a digital image analysis method suited to the resulting crumb. Across two experimental batches, the work produced a coherent and internally consistent picture of BSG behaviour and of the relative merits of the two mitigation strategies.

BSG incorporation confirmed its dual character as both a nutritional asset and a technological liability. Substitution increased the ash, protein and fibre content of the bread and, on the dough side, raised water absorption while sharply prolonging development time, increasing stability and depressing paste viscosity — a profile reflecting fibre-driven rigidity rather than genuine bread-making strength. These changes translated into progressively lower loaf volume, reduced 2D porosity and a finer, denser crumb, and into a steep, dose-dependent decline in sensory acceptance. The replication of the 10% versus 20% acceptance boundary across two independent panel sessions established firmly that, under the present formulation and process, 20% substitution exceeds the limit of consumer acceptance, while 10% remains acceptable on compositional, technological and sensory grounds alike.

The two mitigation strategies proved not to be equivalent. Xylanase reliably improved loaf volume — and at 140 ppm produced the highest volume of the entire study at 10% unsieved BSG — but escalating the dose did not systematically improve sensory acceptance and, where arabinoxylan substrate was limited, worsened it, consistent with the release of bitter phenolics and reducing sugars by excess hydrolysis. Particle-size fractionation, by contrast, traded a small loss of loaf volume for clear and reproducible gains in crumb porosity, cell density and, above all, sensory acceptance. The single formulation that converged on all measurement axes was 10% sieved BSG (≤250 µm) with 70 ppm xylanase: it matched the wheat-flour control in sensory acceptance (85.00 vs. 85.94 points) with the lowest panel variability of the study, approached the control in loaf volume and shape, achieved the best crumb structure of the BSG-containing breads, and delivered a measurable nutritional enhancement of roughly +14% protein and +30% ash over the control. This formulation is therefore identified as the best-performing of the study, and resolves the technological–sensory paradox observed in Batch 1, where xylanase improved volume but degraded flavour when applied to unsieved BSG.

The principal interpretive conclusion is that sensory mitigation in BSG bread is governed primarily by particle-size optimisation rather than by enzyme-dose escalation. Xylanase remains a useful complementary technological lever, most valuable at higher substitution levels where loaf volume is the limiting constraint, but the present data do not justify raising the dose beyond 70 ppm at the 10% inclusion target. Methodologically, the BreadScan digital image analysis pipeline — built specifically for this work with bread-type-adaptive thresholding for dark, fibre-laden crumb — was validated as a non-destructive proxy that tracks both technological and sensory quality, although the imperfect mapping between porosity and acceptance shows that crumb architecture is a necessary but not sufficient determinant of sensory quality and must be read together with chemical and particle-size factors.

Two limitations temper these conclusions. The AOAC crude-fibre method captured predominantly insoluble fibre and underestimated the soluble fraction released by xylanase, leaving the enzyme's contribution to total dietary fibre only partially quantified; and the trained panel of eight limited the statistical power to separate closely scored formulations. Future work should therefore include a comprehensive soluble/insoluble fibre profile, a larger consumer panel, instrumental texture and shelf-life measurements, and a quantification of free phenolics and reducing sugars to confirm the proposed sensory mechanism. Notwithstanding these limitations, the thesis identifies a clear formulation pathway — 10% sieved BSG with 70 ppm xylanase — that achieves its central goal: a protein-enriched, BSG-valorising wheat bread whose technological, structural and sensory quality is comparable to that of a conventional wheat-flour reference, and that demonstrates the practical value of digital image analysis for the objective development of fibre-enriched bread.
