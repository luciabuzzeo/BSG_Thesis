1 Introduction ~2 min

Land this: why BSG matters (scale + waste), why bread, and the one obstacle the thesis attacks — the 10–15% sensory ceiling.

2 The problem: a valuable by-product, wasted ~60 s

Show
  • The three big numbers: 85–90%, 36–39 Mt, 6.4 Mt.
Say
  • BSG is the brewing industry's most abundant solid residue — 85–90% of it, ~36–39 Mt/year worldwide, 6.4 Mt in the EU (~0.2 kg per litre of beer).
  • Mostly landfilled or downgraded to feed — landfilling one tonne releases ~513 kg CO₂-eq — clashing with circular-economy and EU Green Deal goals.
  • Yet it's rich in fibre, protein, minerals and antioxidant phenolics. Recovering it as a food ingredient is both an environmental and a nutritional opportunity.

3 Why bread, and the central dilemma ~60 s

Show
  • Two-column contrast: nutritional upside vs. sensory penalty.
Say
  • Bread is the ideal vehicle — high-volume staple; BSG raises fibre and protein, lowers digestible starch.
  • But the same fibre competes for water and weakens gluten → lower volume, denser/darker crumb; coarse husk → gritty mouthfeel; released phenolics → bitterness.
  • "Acceptance falls sharply once substitution passes about 10–15% — the first quality to fail and the hardest to recover. That ceiling is what this thesis targets."

2 Objectives ~1.5 min

Land this: the specific unresolved gap, one clear aim, and the three testable hypotheses.

4 Research gap and aim ~45 s

Say
  • Two mitigation strategies exist — xylanase (dissolves arabinoxylan fibre, frees water) and particle-size fractionation (sieving removes coarse husk) — but their relative importance and interaction are unresolved, and crumb structure is rarely measured objectively.
  • General aim (one sentence): evaluate how xylanase and fractionation affect the techno-functional, compositional and sensory quality of BSG bread, and build a reproducible image-analysis method — to find a formulation matching a conventional wheat reference.

5 Objectives and hypotheses ~45 s

Say
  • Don't read all six objectives — group them: "characterise composition and rheology, measure technology and sensory across the design, build and apply BreadScan, then integrate everything to pick the best formulation."
State the hypotheses clearly (Conclusions answers them)
  • H1 — quality declines dose-dependently, threshold near 10–15%.
  • H2 — enzyme and sieving mitigate via distinct mechanisms and may act synergistically.
  • H3 — image-analysis crumb metrics correlate with technological and sensory outcomes.

3 Materials & Methods ~2.75 min

Land this: it's a controlled two-batch design, the toolkit is standard and comparable, and BreadScan is your original methodological contribution.

6 Experimental design: two comparable batches ~60 s

Say
  • Batch 1 (baseline, 5 breads): control, 10%, 20% BSG, and 10%/20% + 70 ppm xylanase — all unsieved. This is where the problem revealed itself.
  • Batch 2 (expanded, 9 breads): crosses BSG type (unsieved vs. ≤250 µm fraction) × dose (70 vs. 140 ppm) at 10% and 20%, plus crumb imaging.
  • Identical protocols across both batches, so results are directly comparable.

7 The analytical toolkit ~45 s

Say (fast)
  • Proximate composition · dough rheology (farinograph/amylograph) · loaf volume & shape · trained sensory panel · BreadScan for crumb.
  • Materials in one line: BSG from the Agribiotech micro-malthouse in Nitra (HEINEKEN pale malt), dried and milled; wheat flour T-650; enzyme Pentopan Mono BG, a GH11 endo-1,4-β-xylanase from Aspergillus oryzae.

8 BreadScan: my in-house image-analysis tool ~60–70 s

This is your "wow" slide — slow down
Say
  • Standard white-bread image methods fail on BSG crumb: dark and fibre-laden, so pores and matrix have low contrast and particles mimic pores.
  • Browser-based pipeline: acquire + ruler calibration → CLAHE contrast → adaptive (Sauvola-type) thresholding → morphology + connected-component analysis → metrics: 2D porosity, cell density, mean cell area.
  • "Reproducible, non-destructive, and adapted specifically to this bread type — a methodological contribution of the thesis."

4 Results ~5.5 min — heart of the talk

Land this: (1) the Batch-1 paradox creates tension, (2) Batch 2 resolves it — fractionation, not enzyme dose, drives sensory, (3) crumb data confirm it objectively, (4) one formulation wins, (5) the headline takeaways.

9 Batch 1: the xylanase paradox ~75 s

Show
  • Loaf photos: control → 10% → 20% → +enzyme.
Say
  • 10% is acceptable across composition, technology and sensory; 20% shows clear penalties — wet gluten drops 29.0% → 22.5%, dough stiff and inelastic.
  • The paradox: xylanase on unsieved BSG raised volume (+6.5% at 10%) but sensory dropped — 76.75 → 65.25 points.
  • "No single Batch-1 bread matched the control on both volume and sensory — that's what motivated Batch 2."

10 Batch 2: fractionation drives sensory quality ~75 s

Show
  • Sensory bar chart; point to the acceptance ≈ 70 line.
Say
  • Read the pattern, not every bar: 10% clusters high, 20% fails regardless of enzyme or sieving.
  • 140 ppm maximised volume (up to 1050 mL) but not sensory — more enzyme ≠ more acceptance.
  • Key contrast: at 10%, the sieved fraction scored 85.0 vs. 81.8 unsieved — sieving is what recovers eating quality.

11 Crumb structure (BreadScan DIA) ~60 s

Show
  • Metrics table + control-vs-10% crumb images.
Say
  • Control crumb is open and light — 41.5% porosity; all BSG breads fall to 16–28%.
  • Among BSG breads, 10% fraction + 70 ppm has the highest porosity and cell density.
  • "The image metrics track both loaf volume and sensory — so BreadScan works as a non-destructive screen" (H3 confirmed).

12 The winning formulation ~75 s — the climax

Show
  • Winner-vs-control table + sensory comparison (85.0 vs 85.9).
Say
  • 10% sieved BSG (≤250 µm) + 70 ppm xylanase.
  • Matches the control on sensory (85.0 vs. 85.9, within one SD, lowest panel variability of the study), approaches it on volume (925 vs. 975 mL), best crumb of the BSG breads.
  • Nutritional bonus: +14% protein, +30% ash.
  • "Fractionation plus the original 70 ppm dose achieve together what neither did alone — this resolves the Batch-1 paradox."

13 Key findings ~45 s

Say — four crisp statements
  • Threshold — 10–15% is the ceiling; 20% fails everywhere.
  • Particle size is the decisive sensory lever, not enzyme dose.
  • Xylanase is a volume tool — no benefit beyond 70 ppm.
  • Winner — 10% sieved + 70 ppm matches the control with a nutritional gain.

5 Conclusions ~1 min

Land this: answer each hypothesis explicitly, then give the single interpretive take-home.

14 Conclusions ~60 s

Say — tie back to the hypotheses
  • H1 confirmed — dose-dependent quality loss; 10–15% threshold replicated across two independent panels.
  • H2 confirmed — distinct mechanisms: sieving improves sensory, enzyme improves volume; synergy at 10% fraction + 70 ppm.
  • H3 confirmed — image-analysis crumb metrics correlate with technological and sensory outcomes.
  • Take-home (slowly): "Sensory quality in BSG bread is governed mainly by particle size, not enzyme dose. Xylanase is a complementary volume tool with no benefit beyond 70 ppm at 10% substitution."

6 Recommendations & future work ~45 s

Land this: you know the study's limits, and there's a clear forward path.

15 Recommendations & future work ~45 s

Limitations (honest, brief)
  • Crude-fibre method underestimated soluble fibre released by the enzyme; small panel (n = 8) limited statistical power; volume measured once per bake.
Future work
  • Soluble/insoluble fibre profiling · larger consumer panel · shelf-life & microbial safety · 3D crumb imaging (micro-CT) · scale-up · extend valorisation to other by-products (e.g. malt rootlets).
