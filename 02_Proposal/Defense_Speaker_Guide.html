1 Introduction ~2 min

Land this: why BSG matters (scale + waste), why bread, and the one obstacle the thesis attacks — the 10–15% sensory ceiling.

2 Introduction — the problem ~60 s

On slide — graphics / tables

  • Three maroon stat cards (85–90% · 36–39 Mt · 6.4 Mt); no chart.

On slide — body text (what to type)

  • 85–90% — of all brewery solid waste is brewer's spent grain (BSG)
  • 36–39 Mt — of BSG generated worldwide every year
  • 6.4 Mt — produced in the EU alone each year
  • Today most BSG is landfilled or downgraded to low-value animal feed — at odds with circular-economy and EU sustainability goals. Landfilling one tonne releases ~513 kg CO₂-eq.
  • Yet BSG is rich in dietary fibre, protein, minerals and antioxidant phenolics.
  • → Recovering it as a food ingredient is both an environmental and a nutritional opportunity.

Say — what to address

  • BSG is the brewing industry's most abundant solid residue — 85–90% of it; ~36–39 Mt/yr worldwide; 6.4 Mt in the EU (~0.2 kg per litre of beer).
  • Its usual fate (landfill / feed) clashes with circular-economy goals; landfilling 1 t ≈ 513 kg CO₂-eq.
  • But it is nutritionally rich → recovering it as food is both an environmental and nutritional opportunity.

3 Introduction — why bread & the dilemma ~60 s

On slide — graphics / tables

  • Two columns (green 'upside' box vs maroon 'penalty' box); maroon banner at the bottom.

On slide — body text (what to type)

  • Nutritional upside: + more dietary fibre · + more protein · + less digestible starch · + familiar, high-volume staple
  • The sensory penalty: – bitter, astringent taste (freed phenolic acids) · – gritty mouthfeel (coarse husk) · – darker, denser, less springy crumb · – lower loaf volume
  • (Banner) Overall acceptance falls sharply once BSG substitution passes ~10–15% — the first quality to fail, and the obstacle this thesis targets.

Say — what to address

  • Bread is the ideal vehicle — high-volume staple; BSG raises fibre and protein, lowers digestible starch.
  • But the same fibre competes for water and weakens gluten → lower volume, denser/darker crumb; husk → gritty; phenolics → bitterness.
  • Say the ceiling line slowly — it frames the whole thesis.

2 Objectives ~1.5 min

Land this: the specific unresolved gap, one clear aim, and the three testable hypotheses.

4 Objectives — research gap & aim ~45 s

On slide — graphics / tables

  • Text-only: 'research gap' paragraph + a maroon-bordered 'General aim' box.

On slide — body text (what to type)

  • The research gap: two mitigation strategies exist — xylanase (solubilises arabinoxylan fibre, frees bound water) and particle-size fractionation (sieving removes the coarse, abrasive husk) — but their relative weight and interaction are unresolved, and crumb-structure effects are rarely quantified objectively.
  • General aim: Evaluate how xylanase treatment and particle-size fractionation affect the techno-functional, compositional and sensory quality of BSG-enriched wheat bread, and develop a reproducible digital image-analysis method for crumb structure — to identify a formulation matching a conventional wheat reference and support BSG valorisation.

Say — what to address

  • Two levers exist but their relative weight/interaction is unresolved, and crumb structure is rarely measured objectively.
  • State the general aim in one sentence.

5 Objectives — objectives & hypotheses ~45 s

On slide — graphics / tables

  • Two columns: numbered objectives (left) and three H-boxes (right).

On slide — body text (what to type)

  • Specific objectives: 1. Characterise composition & wet gluten · 2. Assess dough rheology (farinograph, amylograph) · 3. Technology across BSG level × dose × particle size · 4. Sensory acceptance & inclusion threshold · 5. Build & apply BreadScan (crumb DIA) · 6. Integrate all data → best formulation
  • H1 — Dose-dependent quality loss, with a threshold near 10–15%.
  • H2 — Enzyme and fractionation mitigate via distinct mechanisms and may act synergistically.
  • H3 — DIA crumb metrics correlate with technological and sensory outcomes.

Say — what to address

  • Don't read all six objectives — group them (characterise → measure → build BreadScan → integrate).
  • State the three hypotheses clearly; the Conclusions slide answers them one by one.

6 Materials & Methods — experimental design ~60 s

On slide — graphics / tables

  • Two stacked boxes (Batch 1 blue, Batch 2 green) + maroon comparability banner. No chart.

On slide — body text (what to type)

  • Batch 1 — Baseline (5 breads): Control · 10% · 20% BSG · 10%/20% BSG + 70 ppm xylanase (all unsieved).

Say — what to address

7 Materials & Methods — analytical toolkit ~45 s

On slide — graphics / tables

  • Styled table (blue header) 'Parameter | Principle/method'; small materials line above.

On slide — body text (what to type)

  • Materials line: BSG from the Agribiotech micro-malthouse, Nitra (HEINEKEN pale malt), dried 60 °C, milled, sieved ≤250 µm; wheat flour T-650; enzyme Pentopan Mono BG — GH11 endo-1,4-β-xylanase (A. oryzae); analyses at FBFS, SUA Nitra.

8 Materials & Methods — BreadScan ~60–70 s

On slide — graphics / tables

  • Pipeline step chips; BreadScan calibration image (left), segmentation stages (top), metric output (bottom); own-tool caption.

On slide — body text (what to type)

  • Pipeline: Acquire + calibrate → CLAHE / raw → Threshold (Sauvola / peak-mode) → Morphology + CCA → Metrics.
  • Built for the dark, fibre-laden BSG crumb, where standard white-bread methods fail.
  • Outputs: 2D porosity, cell density, mean cell area. Browser-based, reproducible & non-destructive.
  • → A methodological contribution of this thesis.

Say — what to address

  • This is your 'wow' slide — slow down.
  • Explain why white-bread methods fail on dark crumb, then the pipeline, then the outputs.
  • Call it a methodological contribution.

4 Results ~5.5 min — heart of the talk

Land this: (1) the Batch-1 paradox creates tension, (2) Batch 2 resolves it — fractionation, not enzyme dose, drives sensory, (3) crumb data confirm it objectively, (4) one formulation wins, (5) the headline takeaways.

9 Results — Batch 1: the xylanase paradox ~75 s

On slide — graphics / tables

  • Batch-1 loaf photo (5 loaves) left; bullets right; maroon paradox banner.

On slide — body text (what to type)

  • 10% BSG — acceptable on composition, technology and sensory.
  • 20% BSG — clear penalties; wet gluten falls 29.0% → 22.5%; stiff, inelastic dough.
  • Xylanase (70 ppm) on unsieved BSG: volume ↑ (+6.5% at 10%) but sensory ↓ (76.75 → 65.25).
  • (Banner) No single Batch-1 bread matched the control on both volume and sensory → motivates Batch 2.

Say — what to address

  • 10% acceptable; 20% clear penalties (wet gluten 29.0→22.5%).
  • The paradox: xylanase on unsieved BSG raises volume but drops sensory.
  • Punchline: no Batch-1 bread matched control on both → that's why Batch 2.

10 Results — Batch 2: fractionation drives sensory ~75 s

On slide — graphics / tables

  • Native bar chart of 9 formulations (control grey, winner gold, fractions green, unsieved blue), acceptance labels; note bullets right.

On slide — body text (what to type)

  • Chart: Overall sensory acceptance — Control 85.9 · 10%U·70 81.8 · 20%U·70 56.8 · 10%F·70 85.0 · 20%F·70 62.5 · 10%U·140 78.6 · 20%U·140 69.3 · 10%F·140 72.4 · 20%F·140 66.7. (U = unsieved, F = ≤250 µm, number = ppm.)
  • 10% cluster high; 20% fails regardless of enzyme or sieving.
  • 140 ppm maximised volume (up to 1050 mL) but not sensory.
  • At 10%, sieved (85.0) beats unsieved (81.8) — sieving recovers eating quality.

Say — what to address

  • Read the pattern, not every bar: 10% cluster high, 20% fails regardless.
  • 140 ppm maxed volume, not sensory.
  • Key contrast: sieved 85.0 vs unsieved 81.8 at 10% → sieving recovers eating quality.

11 Results — crumb structure (BreadScan DIA) ~60 s

On slide — graphics / tables

  • Crumb-metrics table (blue header) + two crumb photos (control vs 10% BSG); insight box.

On slide — body text (what to type)

  • Table (2D porosity % · cell density · mean cell area): Control 41.5 / 11.0 / 3.76 · 10% fraction + 70 ppm 27.8 / 8.1 / 3.44 · 10% unsieved + 70 ppm 25.7 / 7.6 / 3.38 · 20% formulations 19–23 / 6.0–9.7 / 2.3–3.0.
  • Porosity drops from 41.5% (control) to 16–28% for all BSG breads.
  • 10% fraction + 70 ppm = highest porosity & cell density among BSG breads.
  • DIA metrics track both loaf volume and sensory → BreadScan validated as a non-destructive screen.

Say — what to address

  • Control 41.5% porosity → BSG 16–28%.
  • 10% fraction + 70 ppm best among BSG breads.
  • Validation line: metrics track volume & sensory (H3 confirmed).

12 Results — the winning formulation ~75 s — climax

On slide — graphics / tables

  • Gold headline banner; winner-vs-control table; sensory 85.0 vs 85.9 compare box; maroon resolution banner.

On slide — body text (what to type)

  • Headline: 10% sieved BSG (≤250 µm) + 70 ppm xylanase — matches the wheat control, with a nutritional bonus.
  • Table (Winner | Control): Sensory 85.0 | 85.9 · CV 9.4% (lowest) | — · Loaf volume 925 mL | 975 mL · 2D porosity 27.8% (best of BSG) | 41.5% · Protein +14% vs control · Ash +30% vs control.
  • (Banner) Fractionation + the original 70 ppm dose achieve together what neither did alone — resolving the Batch-1 paradox.

Say — what to address

  • Name it: 10% sieved BSG + 70 ppm.
  • Matches control sensory (85.0 vs 85.9, lowest CV), approaches volume, best BSG crumb, +14% protein/+30% ash.
  • Resolve the tension: fractionation + 70 ppm did what neither did alone.

13 Results — key findings ~45 s

On slide — graphics / tables

  • Three finding rows (label + text) + maroon winner banner. No chart.

On slide — body text (what to type)

  • THRESHOLD — 10–15% is the ceiling; 20% fails on composition, volume and sensory (two panels).
  • PARTICLE SIZE — the decisive sensory lever: sieving ≤250 µm restores acceptance; enzyme dose does not.
  • XYLANASE — a volume tool only; no benefit beyond 70 ppm; 140 ppm can worsen sensory.
  • (Banner) Winner: 10% sieved BSG + 70 ppm — sensory 85.0 vs 85.9, +14% protein / +30% ash. BreadScan validated.

Say — what to address

  • Deliver as four crisp statements — threshold, particle size, xylanase, winner.
  • This is your verbal summary before conclusions.

5 Conclusions ~1 min

Land this: answer each hypothesis explicitly, then give the single interpretive take-home.

14 Conclusions ~60 s

On slide — graphics / tables

  • Three green H-boxes (H1/H2/H3 confirmed) + maroon take-home banner.

On slide — body text (what to type)

  • H1 CONFIRMED — dose-dependent quality loss; 10–15% threshold replicated across two independent panels.
  • H2 CONFIRMED — distinct mechanisms: sieving improves sensory, enzyme improves volume; synergy at 10% fraction + 70 ppm.
  • H3 CONFIRMED — DIA crumb metrics correlate with technological and sensory outcomes.
  • (Take-home) Sensory quality is governed mainly by PARTICLE SIZE, not enzyme dose. Xylanase is a complementary volume tool — no benefit beyond 70 ppm at 10% substitution.

Say — what to address

  • Tie back to the three hypotheses, one line each.
  • Deliver the take-home slowly — it's the thesis's core message.

6 Recommendations & future work ~45 s

Land this: you know the study's limits, and there's a clear forward path.

15 Recommendations & future work ~45 s

On slide — graphics / tables

  • Two columns: 'Limitations' (maroon) and 'Recommendations / future work' (green). No chart.

On slide — body text (what to type)

  • Limitations: crude-fibre near the gravimetric detection limit; soluble fibre released by xylanase not captured; small panel (n = 8); loaf volume measured once per bake.
  • Recommendations / future work: deploy 10% sieved BSG + 70 ppm as a functional fibre/protein bread; soluble/insoluble fibre profiling + assay of free phenolics & reducing sugars; larger panel with formal statistics (ANOVA/Tukey); instrumental texture & shelf-life; 3D crumb imaging (micro-CT); cross-device BreadScan validation; extend to other by-products.

Say — what to address

  • State limitations honestly and briefly.
  • Give the forward path — note the ANOVA/Tukey point (it answers an opponent question).
